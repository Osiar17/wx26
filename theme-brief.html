Understanding
Stewardship

Stewardship is CCC's theme for 2026, inspired by Paul's letter to the church in Corinth, where believers had been arguing over whether it was he or Apollos who was their true leader. Paul reminded them that whether he or Apollos, they were only "servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God."

Back then, everyone knew what a steward was — a manager looking after someone else's property. Think of Joseph in Egypt: he ran Pharaoh's entire empire, yet owned none of it, even while administering great wealth and increase.

Paul asked a question that grounds stewardship: "What do you have that you did not receive?"

Honestly? Nothing. Everything — time, abilities, even our lives — is received from God. And one day, the Owner will ask: what did you do with what I gave you?

The Will That Wrestles

But while the truth of stewardship is simple, living it out becomes complicated — because of another gift God gave us: the freedom of choice. Every single day, we are faced with constant decisions about who gets to call the shots.

Most of the time, we have no problem choosing His way when it aligns with what we already want. The real struggle is when what He wants conflicts with what we want — because it threatens our pride, comfort, or ambition.

Our will quietly resists, convinced of what we might gain by holding on. In those unseen, internal moments, letting go can come with real pain, sorrow, and very little happiness.

But if stewardship is tested in these hidden places — where the loudest arguments happen in the silence of our own hearts — then fixing it must start there too. It begins by trading our secret reservations for one answer.

The Yes of
the Steward

Everything the Owner asks, everywhere, the steward's answer must remain the same: Yes. But it is never that simple.

In Gethsemane, when the night was heavy, the Father's love called for redemption — but at a price: a cross, nails, and raw shame. Yet Jesus said it anyway: Yes.

When His humanity strained against the desire for self-preservation, He didn't romanticise it. He named it: "Not my will…"

When it meant blood — when the thought of it alone was agonising, so intense He sweated blood while the Father's will stood clear — He did not only resist His own will. He embraced the Father's: "…but Yours be done."

Struggling hour after hour, praying over and over, Jesus laid down His life and all it represented. He surrendered completely: "Not my will, but Yours be done."
